    I am an angel investor and Executive Director of the Angel Capital Association (ACA), the world’s leading professional association for angel investors. ACA is focused on fueling the success of accredited angel investors who support high-growth, early-stage ventures, and has more than 12,000 member angels across North America. I know one thing for sure: there is a method to the madness. In shaping ACA professional development programs and public policy advocacy, I have the opportunity to hear firsthand from experienced angels and the ecosystem at large—what works, what doesn’t work, and strategies to consider for everything from getting started as an investor, to finding great deals, to supporting the companies you invest in to growth and exit.     Start Smart: Many Ways To Build Your Investor IQ

    Comment Now
    Follow Comments Following Comments Unfollow Comments

    Angel investing includes a lot of very smart people. But it’s also very complicated. That’s why the wisest angel investors are always on the lookout for smart ways to avoid doing stupid things.  And there are many people who are new to the field and need knowledge fast. That’s why I’m pleased to see new articles, books, and Internet resources to complement what angels learn through networking and on-the-job training.

    My favorite resource these days is Investor IQ, which tests and builds startup investing knowledge for prospective investors and entrepreneurs.

    Created by the Kauffman Foundation, with support from Angel Resource Institute and Angel Capital Association, it is a free resource and starting point for investors and entrepreneurs to uncover the additional resources they may need to be successful.  I think it’s a terrific resource for both accredited angel investors today and will be very helpful to the many new investors involved in equity crowdfunding, once that channel is allowed for unaccredited investors.

    The free online curriculum and video lessons help investors educate themselves on whether investing in startups is right for them, how to invest, where to invest, and the typical ways their money will be used and returned. The videos draw on the successes and failures of thousands of angel investors over the last 15 years, some famous and some not.

    The Kauffman Foundation recommends users start by taking a quiz—designed like a “Buzzfeed” survey – that is cleverly developed to help users learn where they need the most education and then follow links to the right content for them.

    Beyond videos like this, if you’re still looking for more insights, here are some of my favorites.

    How to be an angel investor investor

    • Angel Investing – The Gust Guide to Making Money & Having Fun Investing in Startups: Author and top angel David S. Rose walks through all aspects of angel investing, providing interesting anecdotes and learnings from his own experience. A superb appendix includes investment documents – screening, due diligence, term sheets, etc., as well as a glossary of terms. This is one of the few books written on how to be an angel.
