Remarkable New York City Gems You Want To Find

By Banji Ganchrow in All, Misc August 18, 2015

In the city that never sleeps, there are some gems that are worth exploring.

New York City is chock full of stress. Everyone is always trying to get somewhere fast. Whether it be to the subway, the bus, to and from work; it's actually amazing that there is no shortage of anti-anxiety medications. Though when you take the time to do a little research and find some hidden gems in our beautiful city, you almost forget everything going on around you. So put down the business section, take off those uncomfortable work shoes, loosen your tie, take a deep breath, and take a trip with us to some of the most remarkable places in New York City that you can visit to help re-center yourself.
The Highline is at the top of the list. The main entrance to this little piece of heaven by the west side highway is on 34th street and 12th Avenue. For the record, all the entrances are handicapped accessible. When you begin walking up the path you immediately begin to see that you are entering a different time period. What was once an elevated railroad track is now a serene place where different plants and flowers grow,  and yet you are surrounded by all that is modern and fast paced about the city. Skyscrapers are going up around you, but you only notice them when you look. Otherwise, you are taken by the horticulture, the different benches to relax on, and the art installations around you.
Olafur Eliasson’s The Collectivity Project is a series of white lego structures which guests of the high-line are welcome to rearrange and build. It is on display until September 30, 2015. There is even a part where you can kick off your shoes and frolic in the water that emanates from part of the path (this is intentional, it is not pee..I asked). The walk ends on Gansevoort Street, and when you exit the high-line you are greeted by yet another jewel in the city, the new Whitney Museum.

The Whitney Museum was founded by Gertrude Vanderbilt Whitney, an artist in her own right who also married quite well. The first museum was on West 8thStreet in Greenwich Village. The Whitney’s second home was on 75th Street and Madison Avenue in a Marcel Breuer-designed building. Alas, her new home is a magnificent structure with beautiful outdoor spaces that totally take advantage of the views in New York City. This museum houses works by some of the most influential American artists of our time.
The current exhibit is titled America is Hard to See. It chronicles different time periods in our history and how the art is a reflection of those events. The works range from Andy Warhol and Roy Lichtenstein to Jeff Koons and Donald Moffett. Some of the sculptures displayed in the outside balconies can be sat on and enjoyed. It is truly a marvel that you can't afford to miss. As you walk  and take in the different pieces, you can almost feel your blood pressure lower.

When you are finished being cultured through art, it is time to take in a different part of American culture. Let’s travel uptown to 417 Fifth Avenue where we will find Saturday Night Live The Exhibition. If you are a fan of the show, you will not be disappointed. If you aren’t a fan, you will become one. If you have had a bad day at work and need a good laugh, this journey through the show’s 40 year history will make you very happy. Aside from the irony of seeing Bill Cosby and OJ Simpson as past guests, the rest of the exhibit is both informative and clever. You learn about the day by day process that goes into making this classic comedy. It covers it all, from the writers to the set designers and finally to the actors.
What is most interesting is the photos of all of the past and present cast members; it is a who’s who of incredible talent, many of whom have gone on to have stellar careers while some you look at and scratch your head wondering: “They were on Saturday Night Live? Whatever happened to them?” The few hours you spend here are guaranteed to make you laugh and relax the tension right out of your body. When you are done, there are plenty of places nearby to have a good meal or a strong cocktail.

The Big Apple

Yoko Ono is an original. Her One Woman Show at the Museum of Modern Art exemplifies the very essence of her originality and it is on display in New York until September 7, 2015. In 1971, she had also displayed her work at this museum, but her show was then titled Museum of Modern (F)art. Gotta love Yoko. You begin the journey with a green apple on a podium. This piece is, surprisingly, titled Apple 1966. Rumor has it that John Lennon first fell in love with Yoko because of this piece. Or, perhaps, he was hungry and he really wanted an apple.
Other pieces of interest are Film No.4 which is 15 second shots of various bare behinds which appear to be walking. The piece is to symbolize that sometimes, we feel overexposed in our lives; like we are walking around with our pants down.
Touch Poem is a diary made out of hair. From people’s heads, I made sure to check that out because you never know with Yoko where the hair could come from. In her Painting in 3 Stanzas, there appears to be a vine growing out of a black canvas. Obvious symbolism of life growing out of the darkness, but I had to see if the vine was real, which it is, but I had missed the Do Not Touch sign and the security guard offered to taser me. Moving on…I will not reveal other installations in this exhibit but it is quite interesting, thought provoking, and highly recommended. And when you are done with this exhibit the MOMA always has an amazing collection of art in many different mediums. Culture at it’s best in the heart of midtown Manhattan.
