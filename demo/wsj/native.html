Big Business Meets the Industrial Internet of Things

NARRATIVES BY WSJ. CUSTOM STUDIOS FOR   

Using Data to Achieve Measurable and Meaningful Outcomes

The Industrial Internet of Things (IIoT) is a network of intelligent objects, such as machine sensors or wearable devices, connected to data-collecting systems and platforms. It will be the largest driver of corporate growth, productivity and profitability over the next 15 years, and could add up to $7.1 trillion to the U.S. economy by 2030.1

The IIoT is an integral part of this burgeoning "outcome economy": a shift toward prioritizing the results a product or service provides over the product itself. "Successful companies are beginning to see the importance of selling outcomes—and that requires weaving their businesses into the broader digital fabric that extends to customers, partners, employees and industries," explains Paul Daugherty, chief technology officer at Accenture.

Every industry stands to gain from the IIoT and the outcomes it produces, from mining and manufacturing companies to hospitals and agricultural producers. Whatever your industry, producing successful outcomes begins with understanding the links between the IIoT and the outcome economy, and the emerging technologies that it will create.

Connected devices and measurable results
The hardware and software that comprise the IIoT provide businesses with a constant stream of valuable data about operations, employees and consumers. Companies can collect and analyze this data and apply it to improve the efficiency and effectiveness of all manner of business functions, including sales, marketing, production and distribution.

The look of the outcome economy
Enterprises participating in the outcome economy are already using a mix of hardware and software to gather data and improve operations.

  • A water and wastewater services company uses sensors, analytics and real-time data to respond quickly to leaks or weather-related events.
  • An oil refinery's employees wear wireless devices that detect the presence of poisonous gases. Managers at the plant can monitor employee safety, status and location, and employees can press a help button if they need assistance.2
  • A health systems company uses connected devices to monitor patients for infection risk factors and symptoms, enabling providers to diagnose infection and intervene more quickly.3
  • A global energy company is using analytics to gather data at its wind farms. Thousands of data points collected from the turbines every second allow the company to adjust performance controls like speed, torque and pitch. This fine-tuning has enhanced turbine operation, giving way to 5 percent increases in energy output.4

In each of these cases, the organization was able to capitalize on the Industrial Internet of Things to improve end results: response times, employee safety, speed of diagnosis and energy output, respectively.

Prith Banerjee, Accenture's managing director of global technology and research and development, notes that as the IIoT continues to expand, it will open a new era of economic growth and competitiveness. "Companies moving quickly along the outcome economy maturity curve will outrun their rivals and thrive long term," he predicts.
